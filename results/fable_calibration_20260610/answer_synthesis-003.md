# Distributed Consensus via Raft, at Three Levels

## 1. For a non-technical CEO

Imagine your company's critical records are kept by a committee of clerks instead of a single bookkeeper — if one clerk gets sick or their office burns down, the business doesn't stop, because the others hold identical copies and have already agreed on every entry. Raft is the procedure that committee follows: they elect a head clerk, every change goes through them, and nothing counts as official until a majority has written it down. For the business, this is what lets your systems keep running and your data stay correct through server crashes and outages — it converts "one machine failing" from a crisis into a non-event.

## 2. For a senior backend engineer

Raft keeps a replicated log consistent across a cluster by funneling all writes through a single elected leader. Nodes are followers until they stop hearing the leader's heartbeats; after a randomized election timeout, a follower increments the *term* (a logical clock), becomes a candidate, and requests votes — winning requires a majority, and each node votes at most once per term, so at most one leader can exist per term. The leader accepts client commands, appends them to its log, and replicates entries via `AppendEntries` RPCs that carry the previous entry's index and term; followers reject inconsistent appends, and the leader backs up and overwrites divergent follower suffixes until logs match. An entry is *committed* once stored on a majority, at which point it can be applied to the state machine. Safety comes from two interlocking rules: voters refuse candidates whose logs are less up-to-date than their own (Election Restriction), so any new leader already contains every committed entry; and a leader only directly commits entries from its own term (commitment rule), which closes the window where an old-term entry on a majority could still be overwritten. Together these give you the Leader Completeness and State Machine Safety properties: all nodes apply the same commands in the same order, and committed writes survive any minority of crashes. Practical corollaries: you need 2f+1 nodes to tolerate f failures, writes cost a majority round-trip, and linearizable reads require either going through the leader with a heartbeat/lease check or a ReadIndex protocol — stale reads from followers are otherwise possible.

## 3. For a distributed systems researcher

FLP tells us no deterministic protocol can guarantee consensus termination in an asynchronous system with even one crash failure, so Raft — like Paxos — guarantees safety unconditionally and buys liveness only under partial synchrony. Its escape hatch from FLP is randomization in a weak form: randomized election timeouts break the symmetry that would otherwise allow perpetual split votes, making the leader-election subproblem (a failure detector / Ω-leader-oracle implementation, in Chandra–Toueg terms) terminate with probability 1 once message delays stabilize — note this randomizes scheduling rather than the agreement decision itself, unlike Ben-Or-style protocols. Versus Multi-Paxos, Raft's central design bet is understandability through a *strong leader* and log contiguity: logs never have holes, entries flow only leader→follower, and leadership is coupled to log completeness via the election restriction, so a new leader never needs to learn or reconcile chosen values the way a new Paxos leader must run phase-1 over uncommitted slots. The costs of that simplicity are real: Paxos permits out-of-order acceptance per slot and decouples the proposer from log state, enabling optimizations Raft forbids or makes awkward — e.g., any sufficiently-flexible-quorum schemes (Flexible Paxos applies to both, but Paxos's slot independence gives more room), pipelining around gaps, and electing the best-connected rather than most-up-to-date node. Raft's single-server-at-a-time membership change (or joint consensus) is simpler but less general than Paxos's α-reconfiguration, and its term-based overwrite of divergent suffixes trades the generality of Paxos's value-choice rule for an invariant that's mechanically checkable — which is largely why Raft has accumulated more verified implementations (e.g., Verdi) and production deployments (etcd, TiKV, Consul) despite being formally a restriction, not an extension, of the Paxos family.
